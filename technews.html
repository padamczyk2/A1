Email communication has been a foundation of how internet is used and remains one of the top targets when it comes to cyber-attacks. It is attractive for scammers because of its personal nature – a mine of sensitive information. Nowadays, attackers advance their level of sophistication, making security threats more prominent than ever before. Every business,no matter big or small, is at risk. Sadly, most of them learn it the hard way once the protection mechanisms have been breached.
In this article you will learn a few tips on how to stay safe, but first, let’s take a look at the most common email security risks this year:
Phishing Threats occur when an attacker, pretending to be a trusted sender, lures the recipient to click a link which leads to installation of malware and therefore provide access to sensitive information.

Credential Theft happens when someone steals our credentials, ca. login information, to gain access to critical data and go around company’s security measures.

We talk about fraudulent payments whenever an attacker uses other people’s payment information to make unauthorized purchases.

While the aforementioned scams represent a high degree of randomness as far as the recipient is concerned, you should be aware of attacks targeted at specific people or organizations.

In spoofing, a real email address, phone number and other personal information of the sender are disguised. Displayed information look very familiar, but often times differ by just one letter or symbol – enough to deceive the recipient. The goal of spoofing, compared to phishing, is not to steal just any sensitive information – it’s identity theft. Spear phishing is similarly aimed at a specific person or business.

These techniques comprise into an attack surface, which is a multitude of actions that can be exploited to identify email environment vulnerability. All of this can be done in order to gain what matters the most – sensitive data. Its possession can lead to a great deal of trouble, including financial loss, significant downtime, reputation and Intellectual Property damage. While phishing and credential thefts make the headlines, it should be well known that the majority of data breaches comes from misdirected email messages.

While at the time of writing, there are no security solutions that prevent a 100% of threats with continuous monitoring, good planning and management of the IT infrastructure, a lot can be done. Your tech-savvy colleagues can target and blacklist domains that send spam, implementing solutions like SPF/DKIM/DMARC checking that prevent phishing and data loss. There is also a set of best practices we should all introduce to our everyday routine:

1. Set up and use multifactor authentication wherever possible

2. Avoid simple passwords (like 123456). Opt for strong and unique ones.

3. Remember not to click links in emails from unknown sources.
